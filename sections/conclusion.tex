\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented ATLAAS, the first end-to-end pipeline that automatically lifts RTL-extracted accelerator semantics to tensor-level ISA specifications, addressing the manual specification bottleneck that has prevented automated compiler generation tools from reaching the vast majority of tensor accelerator designs.
By introducing an 8-pass MLIR semantic lifting pipeline---spanning bit-manipulation canonicalization, MAC pattern detection, control-flow specialization, saturation recovery, loop reconstruction, linalg lifting, and structured metadata emission---ATLAAS bridges the abstraction gap between gate-level hardware behavior and the tensor-level semantics required by specification languages like TAIDL.

Evaluated on Gemmini and VTA, ATLAAS processes 156 MLIR files totaling over 1.2~million lines of bit-level IR, achieving up to 92.9\% reduction on compute-dominated modules and recovering hardware features---multi-bank DMA configuration, pooling engine semantics, and im2col support---that were absent from hand-written references.
All lifted semantics are formally verified equivalent to the underlying RTL via Z3 SMT proofs, and the same pipeline generalizes across accelerator architectures without modification.
By feeding the extracted specification into ACT, ATLAAS enables a fully automated path from RTL to a performance-competitive compiler backend, achieving a geometric mean speedup of $1.014\times$ over hand-written kernels on workloads up to ResNet-50 and MobileNet.

More broadly, ATLAAS closes the frontmost gap in the RTL-to-framework pipeline: the step from hardware design to machine-readable ISA semantics that has historically required deep architectural expertise and manual effort.
With this gap filled, the complete automated path---RTL through ATLAAS to TAIDL specification, then through ACT to an XLA-compatible compiler backend---is now possible, lowering the barrier for new accelerator designs to achieve full software-stack integration.
